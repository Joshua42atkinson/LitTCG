**Date:** July 7, 2026  
**Product:** LitTCG — Literary Trading Card Game  
**Status:** Alpha  
**Purpose:** Understand the *why* behind the product, the audience it serves, the ecosystem it competes in, and the concrete needs it must meet before we can sell it.

---

## 1. The Core Problem LitTCG Solves

### The Parent's Pain
Parents today feel **guilty about screen time**. They hand a child a tablet to get 20 minutes of peace, but they worry the time is wasted. Educational apps often feel like thinly disguised worksheets — or worse, they are engineered to extract subscription money from kids with ads, loot boxes, and daily streaks.

What parents want is simple: **screen time that is clearly learning time**, without manipulation, without cloud tracking, and without needing a PhD to understand if it is working.

### The Kid's Pain
Kids resist flashcards. They do not want to memorize vocabulary lists. They want to collect things, battle things, and feel smart. The learning that sticks is the learning they do without noticing they are doing it.

### The Teacher's Pain
Teachers need literacy tools that actually engage students, run on existing hardware, and produce evidence of progress. Most "educational games" are either drill apps with no depth or entertainment games with no learning alignment.

### LitTCG's Answer
LitTCG makes **vocabulary acquisition the game itself**. A child spells a real word, and the word becomes a unique pet. The pet's element, stats, face, and combat moves come from the word's actual meaning. The child is not answering quiz questions — they are collecting a creature.

The parent sees a readable save file and a dashboard showing exactly what words the child learned, how they are using them, and what kind of learner they are becoming.

The teacher gets a standards-adjacent literacy tool that runs on a Chromebook browser and produces concrete evidence of student progress.

**Why this matters:** LitTCG turns the guilty pleasure of screen time into visible, trusted learning.

## 2. The Ecosystem This Game Will Live In

LitTCG does not exist in a vacuum. It must fit into three overlapping ecosystems: **homeschool purchasing**, **school EdTech procurement**, and **children's gaming culture**.

### 2.1 Homeschool Ecosystem
- **Market size:** 3.4 million homeschool students in the US (2024–2025), ~6.3% of K–12.
- **Market value:** $38.6B in 2026, projected $102B by 2035.
- **Buyer behavior:** Parents buy curriculum, supplemental apps, and educational games directly. They research on Facebook groups, Reddit, blogs, and YouTube. Word-of-mouth is powerful.
- **Trust signals:** COPPA safety, no subscriptions, one-time pricing, visible progress, Christian/secular neutrality, offline capability.
- **Distribution:** Direct website, itch.io, Steam, homeschool curriculum fairs, Facebook groups, email newsletters.
- **Pricing tolerance:** Homeschool families regularly pay $10–$50 for quality single-subject tools and $100–$300/year for full curriculum platforms. A $9.99 one-time purchase is low-friction.

### 2.2 K–12 School Ecosystem
- **Hardware:** Chromebooks dominate with 60.1% of K–12 device market share; 93% of US districts planned Chromebook purchases in 2025–2026.
- **Procurement:** Schools buy via district IT, not individual teachers. They need web-based tools, FERPA/COPPA compliance, classroom management, and evidence of standards alignment.
- **Adjacent platforms:** Google Classroom, Google Workspace for Education, ClassDojo, Seesaw, Clever, IXL, Lexia, Prodigy.
- **Buyer behavior:** Teachers pilot free tools; district administrators buy site licenses. Sales cycles are long (3–18 months) but contracts are large.
- **Pricing tolerance:** Schools prefer predictable pricing: per-student, per-classroom, or site-wide annual licenses. They dislike consumer-style subscriptions pushed to parents.

### 2.3 Children's Gaming Ecosystem
- **Reference games:** Pokémon, Yu-Gi-Oh!, Slay the Spire, Prodigy Math, WonderLang, Roblox word games, Hearthstone.
- **Player expectations:** Collection, rarity, evolution, strategic combat, character expression, social sharing.
- **Platforms:** PC, mobile, web, consoles, XR headsets.
- **Monetization norms:** Free-to-play with optional subscriptions dominate, but parents are increasingly hostile to manipulative monetization.
- **Discovery:** YouTube, TikTok, Twitch, Discord, school playgrounds, app store charts.

### 2.4 Parent-Teacher Communication Ecosystem
Products like **ClassDojo**, **Seesaw**, and **Remind** have trained parents to expect a daily or weekly message from school. These apps send:
- Photos of student work
- Behavior reports
- Progress updates
- Two-way messaging with teachers

**LitTCG opportunity:** The game can generate a daily or session-end summary that parents receive — not from a teacher, but from the game itself. This is a new category: *automated learning updates from the child's own play*.

## 5. What Makes LitTCG Special (The "Why Us")

### 5.1 The Intellectual Property: Word Meaning → Creature
This is the core defensible idea. The FACES protocol reads a word's dictionary definition and produces a 4-byte emotional state (38,400 unique states). That state maps to:
- **Aura** → color
- **Container** → head shape
- **Focus** → eye expression
- **Action** → mouth expression

Combined with element (from etymology root) and role (from suffix), every word produces a visually and mechanically unique pet. No hand-modeled assets required. No AI generation latency. No two words are the same.

**Why this matters for marketing:** We can truthfully say, "Spell any word and get a creature no one else has." That is a powerful, verifiable claim.

### 5.2 The Pedagogy Is Invisible
The game does not tell the child they are learning psycholinguistics. It shows them:
- Concrete words ("rock") hit hard.
- Positive words ("joy") have more health.
- Intense words ("rage") are fast.
- Dominant words ("king") defend well.

This is the **VAAM** philosophy: Vocabulary Acquisition through Autonomous Meaning. The child learns by experiencing the word, not by memorizing it.

### 5.3 The RPS Trivium
Three pet classes map to the classical Trivium:
- **Slime** = Logic / Dialectic (semantic analysis)
- **Golem** = Grammar / Structure (roots and suffixes)
- **Robot** = Rhetoric / Persuasion (social combat)

This gives the game instant strategic depth (rock-paper-scissors) while quietly teaching academic concepts.

### 5.4 Parent-Visible Progress
The save file is human-readable JSON. The dashboard can show:
- Words encountered, mastered, and in progress.
- Emergent class (Oracle, Bard, Cultivator, Templar).
- Time spent and grade level.
- Mastery streaks and difficult words.
- Recent quests and battles.

This turns invisible play into a story the parent can understand.

### 5.5 Ethical Design
- No ads.
- No subscriptions marketed to kids.
- No cloud accounts or tracking.
- COPPA-safe by design.
- Local-first save file.

This is a real differentiator in a market where most children's apps are designed to extract recurring revenue.

## 6. The Parent-Teacher Bridge: What an End-of-Day Message Looks Like

This is one of the most powerful ideas. The game can generate a short, readable summary at the end of a play session or school day. This is not a complex dashboard — it is a message.

### Example Message (from the game to the parent)

> **Today's LitTCG Report for Emma**
>
> Emma played for 22 minutes and summoned 5 new pets.
>
> **New words discovered:** thunder, garden, fortress, whisper, joy  
> **Mastered today:** thunder (used in a battle win)  
> **Emergent class:** The Bard (Heart channel growing) — Emma is drawn to emotional and social words.
>
> **Top moment:** Emma spelled "fortress" and got a Rare Earth Tank. She used it to win a quest for Barnaby in the Garden District.
>
> **Suggested conversation:** Ask Emma what "fortress" and "whisper" have in common. (Hint: they both share a suffix pattern!)
>
> No subscription. No ads. Save file lives on your device.

### Why This Works
- It gives the parent **something to talk about** with the child.
- It turns screen time into **family connection**.
- It provides **evidence of learning** without a test.
- It suggests a next step, making the parent feel involved.

### Teacher Version
The same data can be aggregated for a classroom:

> **Class LitTCG Report — Room 204**
>
> This week students played an average of 18 minutes and mastered 12 new words each.
>
> Most common new words: dragon, river, courage, shadow, brilliant  
> Class attunement trend: Body (concrete/physical words) — students are drawn to strong, tangible vocabulary.
>
> Students needing support: 3 students are still struggling with words in the 3–5 grade band. Suggested focus: adjectives and synonyms.

## 7. Needs-Based Assessment: What We Must Deliver

### 7.1 Parent Needs (ranked by urgency)
1. **Trust that the game is safe and educational.** → COPPA-safe, no ads, no tracking, demo available.
2. **Visible progress.** → Dashboard, save file, end-of-day messages.
3. **Affordable, predictable pricing.** → $9.99 one-time, optional annual dashboard.
4. **Works on their devices.** → Web/Chromebook first, then desktop/tablet.
5. **No manipulative monetization.** → No subscriptions pushed at kids, no loot boxes.
6. **Engagement without guilt.** → Game-first design, not worksheet-first.

### 7.2 Teacher Needs
1. **Runs on school hardware.** → Web/Chromebook deployment.
2. **Easy classroom management.** → Roster, assignments, progress reports.
3. **Standards alignment.** → Map words/skills to grade-level literacy standards.
4. **Evidence of student growth.** → Exportable reports, mastery tracking.
5. **Differentiation.** → Automatic grade-level filtering, difficulty scaling.
6. **Low supervision.** → Self-directed play, clear tutorials.

### 7.3 Kid Needs
1. **Immediate fun.** → Pokéball card-flip moment, fast pet spawning.
2. **Agency.** → Choose words, choose pets, choose strategies.
3. **Collection.** → Pet collection screen, rarity, evolution.
4. **Social proof.** → Share rare pets, compare with friends.
5. **Fair challenge.** → Battles and quests that feel winnable but not trivial.
6. **No shame.** → Wrong words become learning moments, not failures.

### 7.4 Market Needs
1. **Differentiation from Prodigy.** → We are not a quiz game with pets; we are a pet game built from words.
2. **Price disruption.** → One-time purchase vs. monthly subscriptions.
3. **Privacy leadership.** → Local-first, no accounts.
4. **Platform flexibility.** → Web, desktop, mobile, XR from one codebase.

## 8. Strategic Implications: What This Means for Sales and Product

### 8.1 Product Positioning
**LitTCG is not an educational app.** It is a **creature-collection tactics game** that happens to teach literacy. This is the only framing that works for kids and parents.

### 8.2 Primary Launch Audience
Homeschool parents ages 6–12 first. They buy directly, they talk to each other, and they hate the things LitTCG avoids (subscriptions, ads, tracking).

### 8.3 Primary Platform
Web/Chromebook. It is the largest education hardware platform and the fastest way to get a demo in front of parents.

### 8.4 Revenue Model
- **$9.99 one-time** full game (main offer).
- **Free demo** with 10 words (top of funnel).
- **$4.99 expansion packs** for SAT, science, Spanish-English.
- **Optional Parent Dashboard** at $29.99–$39.99/year (not monthly) for progress reports and family conversation prompts.

### 8.5 Marketing Narrative
The story is: **"Your kid's screen time just became their vocabulary."**

Pillars:
- **Spell. Summon. Battle.** (the game loop)
- **Every word is a pet.** (the IP)
- **No ads. No subscriptions for kids. One purchase, full curriculum.** (the trust)
- **See what they learned today.** (the parent value)

### 8.6 The XR Role
XR is not the mass-market channel. It is the **proof of premium positioning** and the **viral demo channel**. A short video of a child spelling in XR and a pet bursting out is more shareable than a web GIF. But the revenue engine is web/Chromebook and desktop.

## 9. What We Still Need to Learn

These are open questions that should be answered before major marketing spend:

1. **Age range:** Is the sweet spot 6–9, 8–12, or 6–12? The code supports K–12, but the pet-collection aesthetic may skew younger.
2. **Free vs. paid balance:** Does 10 free words feel generous enough to hook a child, or do we need more?
3. **Parent Dashboard value:** Will parents pay for automated reports, or is the free save file enough?
4. **School readiness:** Do teachers need a full classroom management system, or is a simple web link + export enough for early pilots?
5. **Competitive response:** How will Prodigy and Lexia react if we gain traction? What is our moat beyond the FACES IP?
6. **Localization:** Is the US homeschool market enough, or do we need UK/Australia/Canada adaptations?
7. **Special education:** Could speech therapists or dyslexia specialists become a secondary channel? The FACES/visual-word connection may help here.

## 10. Conclusion: The Why in One Sentence

> **LitTCG exists because parents want their children's screen time to be learning time, kids want their learning time to feel like game time, and teachers want proof that both are happening.**

The game solves this by making words into creatures, making progress visible, and keeping the child's data local and safe. The market is large (homeschool + Chromebook K–12), the competition is weak at this intersection, and the product's core mechanic is genuinely unique.

**The next step is not more marketing theory. It is shipping a stable, polished demo so real parents and kids can tell us what they actually love.**
